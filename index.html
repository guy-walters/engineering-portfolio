During a hands-on engineering internship at a small company producing high-performance engine mounts for AMA Supercross race bikes, I owned the full product development cycle independently, covering reverse engineering, redesign for fitment and stiffness, CAD, CAM programming, CNC machining, and fixture design. Every phase documented here was completed without an engineering supervisor, on live race components used by factory-supported and independent supercross teams.

01 ——

Design & CAD

All engine mount geometry was designed or redesigned from scratch in SolidWorks and Fusion 360. The Ducati Desmo 450 upper engine mounts had pre-existing prototypes when I joined. I identified fitment errors, asymmetry issues, and stiffness problems, then rebuilt the models entirely. The CAD workflow was structured for parametric flexibility so design changes could be propagated quickly without rebuilding from scratch.

Complete CAM program for the Ducati Desmo 450 left-hand upper engine mount in Fusion 360, showing two fully programmed setups with 30+ total toolpaths. Setup2 covers Op2 second-side features using the custom soft jaw fixture. The full program was developed and debugged independently, with toolpath quality benchmarked against a professional machine shop.

SolidWorks — Part Modeling
SolidWorks · Assembly Model — KTM 85 Mount

Full 3D model of the KTM 85 upper engine mount assembly in SolidWorks, showing the swept arm geometry, bolt bosses, lightening slot, and laser engraving fixture plate. Model annotation includes laser focus parameters and part number (KTSM-002). Visual congruence with the company's other parts was a design requirement embedded in the geometry from the start.

The Ducati Desmo 450 mounts I inherited had fitment issues, broken symmetry, and incorrect stiffness distribution. I took physical measurements of the existing prototypes, OEM stock parts, and the bike frame, then redesigned the mounts from scratch, restoring bilateral symmetry, correcting mounting boss positions, and tuning wall thickness to achieve the target stiffness profile.

CAM & Simulation

All toolpaths were programmed in Fusion 360 CAM with custom feed and speed presets developed specifically for the engine mount geometry. Every program was simulated for collision detection and material removal verification before running on the machine. For simple facing operations, G-code was also written by hand directly on the Haas controller.

Fusion 360 — Feed & Speed & G-Code
Fusion 360 CAM · Feed & Speed Parameters

Custom feed and speed preset developed for a bore operation on the engine mount : 4mm carbide ball end mill at 6,000 RPM, 1,219 mm/min cutting feedrate, 0.0508mm feed per tooth, flood coolant. Parameters tuned specifically for the mount geometry rather than pulled from defaults, balancing cycle time against surface finish quality.

Haas Controller · Manually Authored G-Code

Manually authored G-code facing program displayed on the Haas controller, written with full inline comments for readability and shop floor debugging. Program includes absolute mode, metric units, tool change to T6, WCS selection (G59), coolant control, facing passes, and a return-to-door sequence. Written in part to teach the shop owner how to run basic operations independently.

Full Op1 CAM simulation for the Ducati Desmo 450 right-hand upper engine mount, with uncut stock shown in purple and finished surfaces revealed in green as each tool completes its pass. Op2 CAM simulation for the right-hand upper engine mount, showing the part flipped over with remaining stock in purple being cleared to reveal the finished back-face geometry in green. The Haas Super Mini Mill was configured from scratch, with tooling selected and sourced, and tool length offsets and work coordinate systems established independently with no prior shop configuration to reference. Billet stock was measured, marked, and indicated in before each run.

Machining

All engine mount machining was performed on the Haas Super Mini Mill across two operations. Op1 establishes the primary face features: adaptive roughing, profile contouring, bore drilling, chamfering, and parallel finishing. Op2 machines the back face using custom soft jaw fixturing. Toolpath quality was benchmarked against the professional machine shop previously used and achieved comparable surface finish in roughly half the cycle time of previous intern attempts.

Op1 — Primary Face Machining
Video · 2D Adaptive Roughing — 10mm Bullnose End Mill

A 10mm diameter bullnose roughing end mill runs a 2D adaptive clearing operation across the top surface of the engine mount, stepping down to depth and clearing bulk material before finishing passes. The bullnose geometry reduces corner stress and allows aggressive material removal while maintaining tool life.

Video · 2D Adaptive Perimeter Finishing — 5mm Flat End Mill

A 5mm diameter flat end mill runs a 2D adaptive clearing pass around the perimeter of the engine mount, establishing the final outer profile. Adaptive clearing maintains consistent tool engagement to protect the cutter and produce a uniform finish on the contoured edges.

Video · Cylinder Mount Bore Drilling — 12mm Drill

The Haas ATC cycles to a 12mm drill and executes the drilling operation for the primary cylinder mount bore, the largest and most structurally critical hole on the engine mount. This bore interfaces directly with the bike's cylinder, making positional accuracy essential for proper engine alignment on the frame.

Video · Parallel Finishing — 4mm Ball End Mill

A 4mm ball end mill executes a parallel finishing operation across the full surface of the engine mount, making successive X-axis passes over the swept arm geometry, boss transitions, and central lightening slot. Flood coolant runs continuously to manage heat and evacuate chips.

Video · Chamfering — 3mm 45° Chamfer Bit

A 3mm 45-degree chamfer bit breaks the inner and outer edges of all mounting holes on the engine mount. Chamfering deburrs entry and exit edges to prevent stress concentration, eases hardware installation, and gives the finished part a clean, intentional appearance consistent with a race-quality component.

Haas SMM · Desmo 450 — Op1 In-Process

Ducati Desmo 450 upper engine mount mid-Op1, clamped between steel Glacern soft jaws on parallels with flood coolant running. Parallel finishing passes are visible across the face and swept arm geometry.

Haas SMM · Op1 — ATC Tool Change In-Process

The Haas Super Mini Mill's 10-tool ATC carousel visible in frame during a tool change between operations. The engine mount remains clamped in the soft jaws while the machine indexes to the next tool, demonstrating the automated tool management that makes the 30+ operation program practical on a single setup.

Haas SMM · Op1 — Boss & Frame Bore Geometry

View from the opposite end of the machine showing the boss and frame bore geometry mid-operation, confirming the part is holding position correctly through the full operation sequence. Mounting bosses and bore are fully formed at this stage; remaining passes complete the surface finish.

Op2 — Second Side Machining
Video · Op2 — Y-Axis Parallel Finishing, Custom PLA Soft Jaw Fixturing

Second operation on the Ducati Desmo 450 upper engine mount, with the part rotated 90 degrees in the XY plane and clamped in custom PLA soft jaws printed to match the Op1 geometry exactly. A parallel finishing pass runs in the Y direction, contouring the back face of the mount. Using the Op1 geometry as the fixture datum ensures the two operations are referenced to each other, maintaining dimensional accuracy across both sides without a dedicated machined fixture.

Fixtures & Prototypes

A core part of my mandate was bringing prototyping in-house to eliminate the cost and inflexibility of outsourcing. 3D printed prototypes validated geometry before cutting metal. Custom fixtures, both printed and CAD-designed, were created to hold parts for second operations and laser engraving, replacing ad-hoc setups with repeatable, geometry-matched tooling.

3D Printed Prototypes — Ducati Desmo 450
3D Print · Desmo 450 LH & RH Upper Mount Prototypes

Left and right-hand 3D printed prototypes of the upper engine mounts for the Ducati Desmo 450 AMA Supercross race bike, printed in-house on the Bambu Labs P1S. Prototypes validated hole spacing, boss geometry, and overall fitment before committing to machined aluminum, eliminating the batch size minimums and costly design change iterations of the previous outsourcing model.

3D Print · Desmo 450 Prototypes — Second Angle

Second view of the left and right-hand Desmo 450 prototype pair showing the bottom face geometry and frame mounting bore. The bilateral symmetry restored during the redesign is visible here. Both parts are mirror images as intended, correcting the asymmetry present in the pre-existing prototypes.

Profile view of the Desmo 450 printed prototype showing the swept arm geometry and cross-sectional thickness. The layered FDM texture makes wall thickness and material distribution clearly visible, which was useful for communicating stiffness intent and identifying areas needing revision before machining.

3D Print · Prototype — Side Profile

Side profile of the Desmo 450 LH prototype alongside the RH mount, showing the full arm length and boss height. Used to verify that the arm geometry would clear surrounding frame and engine components before cutting aluminum.

Finished Parts

The finished Ducati Desmo 450 upper engine mounts represent the complete design-to-production cycle: an inherited prototype with fitment and symmetry issues, redesigned from scratch, prototyped in-house, and machined to production quality ready for fitment verification and track use.

Op1 Complete — As-Machined Aluminum
Machined 6061 Al · Desmo 450 — First vs. Second Attempt, Top View

First and second attempts at the Ducati Desmo 450 LH upper engine mount, shown side by side. The left part is the original inherited design machined on the first attempt. Note the coarser parallel finishing passes, roughly rounded edges on the bolt holes, and less defined surface geometry. The right part is the redesigned version I developed independently, featuring a recentered relief slot, thinned cross-section for reduced stiffness and weight savings to improve front wheel grip, corrected fitment geometry, and a significantly finer surface finish with visibly tighter parallel pass spacing. Chamfered bolt hole edges on the redesign replace the approximate radii of the first attempt.

Machined 6061 Al · Desmo 450 — First vs. Second Attempt, Frame Bore View

Lower arm and frame mounting bore comparison between the first attempt (left) and the redesigned second version (right). The redesigned part shows cleaner boss geometry, a more uniform scallop pattern from tighter parallel finishing passes, and precisely chamfered bore edges. The thinned arm profile of the redesign is visible here, a deliberate stiffness reduction to allow more chassis flex and improve front wheel traction.

Machined 6061 Al · Desmo 450 — First vs. Second Attempt, Isometric View

Isometric comparison of both mount iterations. The flat top surfaces on the redesigned part (right) are notably cleaner. The facing operation produced a true flat datum, contrasted against the rougher top surface of the first attempt. The finer parallel pass spacing on the redesigned part produces a surface finish approaching the quality of the independent machine shop previously used for prototyping, achieved through refined toolpath parameters and improved fixturing.

Comparison · 3D Print vs. Machined Aluminum — Face View

Direct side-by-side comparison of the 3D printed Desmo 450 LH upper engine mount prototype (black PLA) against the machined 6061 aluminum production part. The prototype validated hole spacing, boss geometry, and arm profile before any material was cut. The machined part shows the counterbored boss faces and surface finish quality achievable on the Haas SMM.

Comparison · 3D Print vs. Machined Aluminum — Back View

Back-face comparison showing Op2-machined features on the aluminum production part relative to the printed prototype. Counterbores and back-side clearances are fully resolved on the production part.
